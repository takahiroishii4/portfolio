August - December, 2018 • Project at UT Austin

Trek project

Design of a mobile app to provide a feeling of safety and enjoyment to outdoor activity lovers

My Main Role Contextual Analysis, Sketching, Mockups, Prototyping
Members Sara Merrifield, Harika Bommu

Summary

[Problem] People struggle to find updated and integrate safety information about trails, including trail conditions and crime info.

[Solution] I designed Trek, a mobile application that allows active individuals to ensure their safety while participating in outdoor activities. It offers planning and safety functions so users can enjoy the outdoors free of the distractions of technology.

[My Role] I was involved in all the steps, and especially contributed to Contextual Analysis, Sketching, Mockups, and Prototyping.

Contextual Inquiry & Analysis

We conducted onsite interviews and observations to understand about out-door activities. We organized the notes into categories: enjoyment, activity pre-planning, safety (perceptions, concerns, actions), technology and observations. Our work affinity diagram (WAAD) is below.

Work Affinity Diagram

Based on the WAAD and the major / machine roles, we generated our flow model.

The major work roles are:

  • Runner / Hiker / Jogger / Camper
  • People who have interactions with the person
  • People who can provide the safety information
  • Emergency personnel including 911 (outside scope)

The machine roles are:

  • GPS tracking
  • Music player
  • Weather tracking
  • Keeping time
  • Recording routes

Design Requirements

Based on our previous findings, we created design requirements. We applied a framework, in which we consider quotes, interactions, and features in this order.

  1. [Planning/Updating] Access to current trail / route conditions
  2. [Planning/History] Recording and accessing routes / trails
  3. [Activity Mode] Distraction free from technology during activities
  4. [Safety] Keeping family/friends (trusted sources) informed
  5. [Safety] Physical safety and emergency situations

Models

Social Model: Safety is an emotional subject. We created a social model, because it centers around the “feelings, issues and concerns” and how they affect their behaviors in this context.

Hierarchical Task Inventory: helped us map out user goals. It allowed us to talk through each task and understand the system. See the full document to see more.

Persona

We spoke to a number of runners at the Town Lake trail and through our interview process. We felt that the persona of Gwen represented a good number of our users who could utilize Trek’s features to plan safer runs. Our interviews revealed discussions of how family and friends expressed safety concerns so we wanted to include a persona that exemplified this. She represents the solo female runner who enjoys her outdoor activities but is also concerned with personal safety.

Sketch

We found the hierarchical task inventory model helpful as we considered how to conceptualize screens. Together we brainstormed and identified important main features and functions of the app that helped us refine our sketching process.

On our app:

When a user plans an activity, they can look up a new or existing route where they can access information such as weather or safety. This information is automated and updated often from various outside sources. A user can also track their own route which utilizes GPS tracking technology. Users can send updates to trusted contacts, like when they expect to complete an activity. Once they select a route they can start their activity and enter ‘activity mode.’

When a user updates trail or route information, they start by searching the route they want to enter information on. They can add comments, update difficulty information or any other information about how weather has affected the conditions.

When a user looks up their past history of activities, they can view a comprehensive list of their outdoor activities and filter by things like location, type of activity and date.

Designer’s Mental Model & Conceptual Design

Ecological Perspective: Our app consists of users’ personal portals but is also part of a larger network of Trek users and other outside sources. It has three main functions: planning an activity, updating trail/route information and looking up your history of activities.

Interaction Perspective: The user will physically use the phone when completing tasks like planning a route or looking up trail information but can either put their phone away so they can fully enjoy their outdoor activity.

Emotional Perspective: We envision our app to allow users to enjoy the outdoors without the burden of worrying about personal safety. The user has the ability to update trusted contacts with information on their wellbeing which instills a sense of security for both parties.

Storyboard

In order to learn the context of users' interaction with our app, we conducted a quick storyboard making session before starting designing the details of the screens. This proved to be an effective exercise as we identified main scenarios together but then were able to bring more specific details. I show one example here, in which a user plans to go running in a place where he/she often goes, but he/she noticed that a crime just happened there. Then, he decided to plan to go to a different place on the app.

Wireframe & User Testing

Based on the previous steps, we created our low fidelity designs with some user testing senssions. This one is our final lofi design.

To refine our lofi designs, we conducted user testing with five participants. An example of the changes is shown.

  1. On our initial lo-fi prototype, the circles on the top were bigger so that one of our participants tried to click one of the circles to progress the planning process. Thus, in order to show the progress, but not to let users click them, we made the circles smaller.
  2. On our initial lo-fi prototype, we worded "Existing Route" instead of "Past Trails", but most of the participants were confused about it. So, we decided to change the wording to "Past Trails"
  3. One of our participants was confused about how these activities shown were sorted. Thus, we decided to enable users to sort by different ways. (chronological - default, frequency, time length of the activity, etc)

You can interact with our lofi design here. Sample tasks are:

  1. Log-in
  2. Plan an activity
  3. Add a trusted contact and begin activity
  4. Update the current condition
  5. View your activity history

Heuristic Evaluation & Cognitive Walkthrough

We performed heuristic evaluation and coginitive walkthroughs with other students to evaluation our prototype and move to the high fidelity. Based on the evaluation, we found some problems on the "chosen route" screen for example. The problems are:

  1. The screen is too crowded and has too much information.
  2. Confused about the difference between "Review" and "Recent Condition", because these had similar UI.
  3. Confused about whether "Recent Conditions" and "Reviews" is selected

To solve these problems, we made some changes on the hifi as shown in the screens below.

  1. By adding more visual elements, icons and colors, I avoided having too much texts in one screen.
  2. For "Recent Conditions", I put my visual elements to inform users about the conditions, while "Reviews" appears like Twitter, so users can understand these are thoughts of other users.
  3. By adding colors and inner shadow, we tried to make it obvious which button is selected. (Recent Conditions / Reviews)

High-fidelity Prototype

We finally moved our high-fidelity prototypes. Here are some examples. For the "plan" function, users can choose a route, check the conditions, and then decide whether to go or not. For the "update", users can update the condition by themselves. There are two types of updates: recent conditions and reviews (their opinions about the trails). For the "history", users can see the list of their past activities. By clicking one of the photos, users can access the details.

What I learned from this project.

At the very first stage of this project, even though we all agreed to create an app for "safety", I was thinking safety in the urban setting while my teammates thought more about safety in nature. This difference made the progress slower at the beginning, so I think we should have spent more time on the initial concept and agreed on the trajectory of the project. However, our onsite contextual inquiry including interviews and observations helped me a lot to understand the current condition and the scope of this project.

For the ideation and design stages, I struggled with what functions our app should include and what to ignore. Since our topic was very broad (safety and enjoyment of outdoor activities), it was not easy for me to conclude which specific painpoints we would address. Based on the models, persona, and storyboards, we decided to focus on "trail conditions", and so in our app, users can see the current conditions easily at the time of planning and doing activities. However, through the user testing, participants were confused about some of the concepts, such as "update". Thus, I learned the importance of user testing to fill the gap between designers and users mental model.

"Trek" was a fun project. I hope I will have another chance to do an outdoor activity related project someday.

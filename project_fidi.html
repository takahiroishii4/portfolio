September - December, 2018 • Project at UT Austin

FiDi project

UX design for phones and wearable devices to monitor, manage, and improve fitness & dietary habits.

Topic UX design process
Members Bhavya Sharma, Peace Iyiewuare, Wendi Xu, Yiping Zhou
Place iSchool at the University of Texas at Austin

Concept Statement

FiDi is a diet and fitness tracking app both for smartphones and wearables. Our vision was to seamlessly integrate both the diet and fitness aspects of healthy lifestyle. Even though the diet and fitness habits are closely related, many products available in the market care only one, but ignores the other. Thus, FiDi aimies to integrate the two aspects, and provide a cohesive experience for individuals interested in maintaining a healthy lifestyle.

We utilized wearable technology to enable users easily monitor, manage, and improve their diet and fitness habits. Currently, wearable devices are advertised with two key benefits: fitness and convenience. With this trend, we aimed to integrate the convenience of tracking fitness and dieting habits within a wearable device, in addition to a smartphone application.

Competitive Analysis

With a competitive analysis, our goal is to better understand how these applications solve problems related to fitness and dietary wellness, and integrate their strengths and unique features into our own design. Please check the full document to see the details, but some of key findings are:

  • Diet apps usually have a good way to collect diet data and provide related tips for the users, while fitness apps usually do not integrate diet information within the app.
  • These apps utilize achievement features, social connection (friends & community), goal settings, and motivational quotes to motivate the users.
  • Not all apps are compatible with a variety of wearable devices, which may cause inconvenience to the user.

User Interviews / Surveys / Analysis

We conducted user interviews to learn descriptive details about participants’ fitness and dietary habits, pain points they might have, and any technology (wearables) or methods they might use to monitor their wellness habits. A total of 10 interviews were conducted across one week. Each interview lasted between 30 minutes and 60 minutes. Between the users interview, we had an near even split of male (n = 6) and female (n = 5), but our participants did skew younger (M = 5). Based on the interviews, we did empathy mapping for fitness, diet and wearable as shown below.

[Fitness] Most participated in some form of physical activity, usually a form of cardio (running, swimming, treadmill, ect...). Additionally, participants had a variety of methods to monitor their fitness habits, including notebook style apps and wearable devices. As for motivation, users primarily noted physical appearance (skinniness/gaining muscle) and social factors.

[Diet] Nearly all participants mentioned being somewhat cautious of their eating habits, and attempting to eat healthy foods. Some participants kept track of what they ate mentally, while others resorted to using apps to track what they ate. Cooking meals at home was a prominent trend, as most participants made home cooked meals multiple times a week. When asked about motivation for eating healthy, weight loss, general health, and pre existing conditions were cited as motivators. Additionally, users didn’t always care about hitting a specific goal when it came to eating healthy.

[Wearables] About half of the participants who we interviewed currently or previously owned a wearable device. Of those, the majority of them used the device to monitor their activity throughout the day. When asked about the potential uses and benefits of owning a wearable device, they cited convenience, activity tracking, and data visualization as the main benefits. One user mentioned owning one made them care more about their fitness.

[Surveys] We also got 52 responses from our online surveys on Qualtrics. The majority of our findings are consistent with our qualitative analysis. Specifically, we found that wearable devices, though not very prominent, are significant enough to warrant designing for that market. Additionally, we see dissatisfaction was more prominent with diet apps compared to fitness apps. Survey data showed lack of motivation, difficulty forming habits, and difficulty tracking meals as major pain points experienced by users.

Persona

With all the data gathered from the research, we aimed to represent our results using personas. These personas would be aggregations of all needs, pain points, preferences, and habits uncovered through our interviews and survey.

Design Requirements

We generated requirements and brainstorm feature sets. This was a collaborative process that involved isolating all needs, pain points, and preferences mentioned in the research, reducing our list to the important requirements (thanks “Deep Dive”), and then brainstorming potential solutions for the list. We also made sure to tie each requirement to the quote(s) it was pulled from. Some examples are below.

  1. Motivators to help users stay on track and meet their goals
    • [Quote] "(When I was using a monitoring app,) I felt I was too lazy to put my notes on the app"
    • [Feature] Not rewarding (rewarding cannot create sustainable motivation and it costs a lot to buy some gifts for users.)
    • [Feature] By showing their progress in a good way (data visualization / virtual madels...), users feel accomplished.
    • [Feature] Community points/support / Sending motivational quotes and messages

  2. Users can easily and quickly monitor their eating habits
    • [Quote] "Failed to monitor. Too inconvenient to quantify the scale of food", "I liked that the app had a scan option"
    • [Feature] Scan foods with pictures or barcodes / Find foods from restaurants / Add fixed recipes / Add manually

  3. Users can monitor a variety of workouts easily
    • [Quote] "Note app was not a good place to organize work-out menus"
    • [Feature] Automatically track running, swimming, biking, yoga, ect...

  4. Users can know what they should do, and if they are doing in the right way
    • [Quote] "I am not sure if I am doing a right work-out with the right amount in the right way"
    • [Feature] Our app can suggest the exercise (and also fitness events in the city)
    • [Feature] Our include action guide for different exercises and sense their movement so that can judge how they are doing

  5. Users can see their progress with beautiful visualization
    • [Quote] "I really liked the visualization in data"

  6. Users can see the recommended recipes on what I buy
    • [Quote] "If the app could know what and how much I eat, I will keep using it."
    • [Feature] Recommendation of recipes / menus / restaurants based on personal habits, health state, and food items in the fridge

Low Fidelity Design & User Testing

Through the iterative process of designing and user testing, we refined our lo-fi UI design for both smartphones and wearable devices. Some of the examples are shown below.

Descriptions for the next three screens.

  1. Users register their personal information for sign-up
  2. The app shows the progress on each fitness activity and each diet factor today
  3. Users can see the detail of their fitness activities

  1. Users can see the details of their diet habits
  2. Users can add a food (meal) for diet or start a fitness activity
  3. During the fitness activity, some date and the bio indicators are shown
  4. When users scan a food, the app shows the size and nutrition for a record

Keeping the usefulness and limitations of wearable devices in mind, we also designed UI for wearable devices. On the wearables, users can start a fitness activity or scan a food for a diet record.

User Testing Results

We conducted three rounds user testing (9 participants in person + 12 on UserTesting.com). They were provided 7 tasks as shown on the right, and we observed how they complete tasks, and how they like our design. Here is some examples of the takeaways.

Home Screen

  • The progress timespan was not useful for users. To solve the issue with timespan used, we used a design similar to Apple Health, where users have the option to switch between day, month, and year using buttons.
  • The progress indicators were unclear. To solve this, We made some changes to the icons used on the progress indicators to better represent them to the users.

Adding food

  • Users first instinct to add a food is to search & Users look into the fitness and diet tabs to add food as well instead of clicking "+" at the icon bar. To solve this issue, we added functionality of "adding" from the fitness and diet tabs at the top right of the screen

Diet & Fitness Suggestions

  • Users looked for recommendations under the diet / fitness tabs and on the community page. To solve this, we decided to include diet and fitness recommendations in both community and their respective tabs.

What I learned from this project.

Throughout the entire UX design process, I could have a practical experience of how to comprehensively and logically connect research, ideation, sketching, designing and user testing. As FiDi focuses on a very broad topic - fitness and diet -, it was not easy to narrow down our scope and decide the design requirements. And on the user testings, many participants were confused about where to find the desired functions, because it can be in the fitness, the diet, or the community section. But finally I think our app successfully integrates both of the aspects of diet and fitness, and provides a comprehensive experience about users’ health.

I personally enjoyed designing functions and screens for wearable devices. Since the screen is small, the possible functions are limited, but wearable devices are more convenient and accessible for users. I learned the importance of understanding the contexts of how users interact with wearables, and providing the appropriate functions for the situations with minimal UI designs.

Overall, we could spend appropriate time on each of the phases, and iterate the design and user testing several times. These processes had made our design solutions more effective and efficient. If I could have another chance to do a similar project, I would focus on a more specific pain point, instead of focusing on all the problems that our project tried to achieve.

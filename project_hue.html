April - June, 2017 • Project at UW HCDE

HUE project

Design of a mobile app that allows entry level workers in a tech firm to find a suitable mentor.

Topic UX design process
Members Vrishti Bhowmik, Warisha Soomro, Mohamed Mohamed
Place HCDE at the University of Washington

Problem Statement

Retention rates among tech　companies for entry-level employees are at an all time low. New hires tend to quit within two years because they do not feel represented, especially when they are assigned roles, given critique and lack of mentors wanting to connect with them in order to help them obtaining a fulfilling career.

Vision Statement

The new design of HUE will help users (especially, underrepresented minorities) feel heard and represented by allowing users to find mentors who can guide them with their careers and remain within their respective companies. This will dramatically improve employee retention rate and lead to more job fulfillment. ​

Background

One of group members who is an officer of National Society of Black Engineers (NSBE) mentioned that he was contact with Sam Schillace, a V.P. of Engineering at Google. Mr. Schillace brought it to our attention that retention rates among underrepresented women and minorities are at an all time low for retention not only at Google but the entire tech industry.

Market Research

  • 8 in 10 employees underrepresented minorities and women who left their tech jobs reported experiencing unfair behavior / treatment.
  • In the tech industry women experienced and observed that they dealt with far more unfairness than their male coworkers.
  • Nearly a quarter of underrepresented minorities and women of color experience daily stereotyping, twice the rate of white and Asian men and women.
  • Limeade can be our primary competitor.

User Interviews

We conducted face to face interviews with entry-level engineers in tech companies to learn the real situation. It revealed that the engineers shared common ideas about their expectations and challenges in a professional setting. Issues that came up included a lack of responsibility over big tasks and feeling confined to certain social groups based on minority grouping.

Persona

Following the research conducted through competitive analyses, interviews and online forums, the team grouped commonalities using an affinity diagram (on the right) to identify key behaviors and motivations of our target users. Then, based on the affinity diagram, we careated personas in order to establish user needs to come up with the appropriate design choices and requirements.

Design Requirements

  • Provide mentorship to tech workers
  • Provide a space for mentees to feel heard regarding issues in the workplace
  • Offer mentees an opportunity to visualize their progress and achievement in order to feel achieved
  • Anonymize identities of mentors and mentees to talk about sensitive issues. Identities can be revealed with consent of mentor and mentee

Initial User Testing

Next, we conducted this user test in order to better understand the users' mental model. By observing and asking how each user completes the three tasks, we can get more insight as to how we can improve the design and experience of our application.

We gave our participants three tasks to complete on our app. Summaries of the three tasks are described below:

  1. Finding a mentor that has a similar trajectory to the mentee user.
  2. logging a brief progress report on a goal they’re currently working on.
  3. Personally messaging / replying to a mentor.

Some of the findings are:

  • Users would like to see a more conventional manner of navigation throughout screen in the application. What we failed to realize was that within each screen the cues (our icons) used for navigation are inconsistent
  • Users would be confused with the idea of “mentorship request”. In our original design, users have to send a mentorship request to get connected with mentors or mentees, but before sending a request, users can have a chat with the potential mentors or mentees to get to know if the person really matches to the user’s need. Main confusitions are:
    1. If users can talk to mentors/mentees before get accepted, they do not need to send the request.
    2. Even though this app is perfectly anonymous, how do they decide whether to accept or not.
  • Too much information on the progress page. Our pages have texts for showing their goals, their logs, and the comments from their mentors. Removing some text and adding graphs or graphics might be helpful.

Annotated Wireframes

Here, using Marvel, we have generated some annotated wireframes that represent the final structure of the features. The one shown below is the flowchart of the screens to sign up and find a mentor. As mentees create a new account, they will answer a set of questions that will help them find their list of best match mentors. The list will provide profiles of mentors, in which mentees can view and request mentorship from.

The messaging page is an opportunity for mentors and mentees to connect on a much more persona level. This is where mentees can ask for advice, gain more knowledge from mentors' experiences, express concerns regarding the workplace environment or even personal development.

Mentees can track their progress in the company and keep adding progress logs to the goal they are working towards. These logs are accessible to mentors, who can provide advice and suggestions as comments.

This is a discussion board. Users can express their opinions anonymously, so that they can exchenge their thoughts on the same problems together.

High-fidelity Prototype

We finally created hi-fi prototypes. I am going to show four of our main prototypes below.

  1. [MENTOR PROFILE] Users can access the profile of a mentor from the list of best matches. The mentor profile includes the following features: Mentorship rating from previous mentees / Response rate / Mentor's career path / Mentor's Goals / Mentor's expertise / Testimonials / Request mentorship button / Message button
  2. [TRACK PROGRESS] Mentees can set goals and track them under the guidance of a matched mentor. This way, progress in a company can be visualized and discussed in order to encourage personal and professional development.
  3. [MESSAGES] This is where mentees and mentors can comfortably hold conversations. They can have conversations about work, seek and provide advice, express concerns, and exchange thoughts.
  4. [DISCUSSIONS] This feature exists in order to provide tech workers a space to share professional experiences that others could benefit from. The whole idea is to create an environment (like social media) that ensures workers that they are not alone in facing workplace issues.

What I learned from this project.

As our app focuses on work environments, and encourages users to express their thoughts on sensitive issues, I personally had a difficult time to consider how to ensure users to feel safe to use our app. Since the user research, I tried to be careful to achieve confidentiality and feeling of connection at the same time through our app. In my opinion, we successfully created the mentorship function, the progress visualization, and the discussion board to tackle the users' main pain points.

If I were to do this project again, I would consider more about how to encourage both mentors and mentees to keep using our app. If users cannot see their mentors / mentees in person, it may not be easy to stap motivated to send messages. Thus, I would think of a reminder function of sending following messages, or a suggestion function to meet up in person. In addition, for our hi-fi prototype, I decided to use distinct colors to imply "inclusion of diversity", but it turned out some people feel distracted to see strong colors fighting again each other on the screens. Therefore, I would also change the color theme next time.

The HUE project was overall very successful, and I enjoyed going through the whole process. It also provided me a valuable opportunity to connect users' insight to design solutions.
